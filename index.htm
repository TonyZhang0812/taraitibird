New Zealand’s Rarest Bird
- Tara Iti
Tony Whitehead - WildLight Photography

The Tara iti is a small, dainty coastal tern named after the beings in Māori mythology who bridge earth and sky. As the rarest breeding bird in Aotearoa, it is threatened in our environment.

About Tara Iti
Its characteristics and current status

Tara iti is a small, dainty coastal tern, characteristic with pale grey upperparts and white underparts, with the rump and forked tail also white. The wings have a dark grey outer web on the outer primary, and breeding adults have a completely yellow-orange bill, and a black cap covering the crown and nape. Tara iti is mainly distributed along North Island coasts and the eastern parts of the South Island, particularly between Whangārei and Auckland. Once widespread, breeding is now restricted to five coastal sites: Waipū, Mangawhai, Te Ārai, Pākiri and Papakānui Spit. Outside of the breeding season, they usually flock at sites in the Kaipara Harbour. As one of New Zealand's rarest native bird species, it only has a population of fewer than 40 individuals, including eight to ten breeding females and approximately nine breeding pairs.

Becoming Endangered
How did tara iti become critically endangered?

The tara iti range contracted during the 19th and 20th centuries due to introduced predators such as rats, cats, hedgehogs and mustelids preying upon eggs and chicks, and the modification of coastal habitats and human disturbance of change to the natural coastal environment, such as the degradation of sand dune habitat caused by residential development, the planting of pine plantations, and pastoral farming. Recreational activities on their habitat and breeding beaches such as drone use, dog walking, vehicles and horses on the beach, and bonfires also disturbed the birds and scare them away from their nests during breeding.

Our Awareness and Efforts
What can we do?

Tara iti has become critically endangered due to threats from predators, the modification of coastal habitats, human disturbance and recreational activities. These threats are related to us and our environment, which means we can take responsibility for helping the tara iti by:

Auckland Zoo
  • Minimizing human disturbance by keeping a safe distance from nesting sites and avoiding loud noises.
  • Keeping dogs, vehicles and horses away, and following local regulations when visiting coastal areas.
  • Do not light fires on conservation land.
  • Do not use drones over public conservation land.
  • If fishing, remove bait or fish remains from the beach to deter the scavenging karoro.
  • Take an active interest in the birds' welfare and supporting conservation efforts by such as donating to protect the tara iti and its habitat, encouraging other people to care as well!
Conclusion

Tara iti, the most endangered species in New Zealand, reminds us that raising awareness is not only our responsibility to tara iti, but also important for protecting our environment and the diversity of our ecosystem. By taking the efforts, we are not just helping tara iti; we are also preserving the natural world we all live in.
